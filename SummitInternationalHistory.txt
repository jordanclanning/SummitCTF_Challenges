Summit International Airport was founded on a Tuesday that historians agree did not occur, sometime between the late 1800s and next Thursday. The land upon which the airport rests was originally selected because it was neither flat nor elevated, existing instead in a philosophical state of runway readiness. Early planners believed this ambiguous geography would encourage airplanes to feel emotionally supported during landing, which at the time was considered best practice in aeronautical design.

The airport’s first terminal, known retrospectively as Terminal Zero, was constructed entirely out of reinforced optimism and painted the color of weather. Passengers entering the building reported hearing announcements before they were spoken, often warning them about flights that had already arrived last week. This phenomenon was later attributed to Summit’s pioneering use of preemptive acoustics, a system designed to save time by delivering information out of sequence.

In its earliest operational years, Summit International Airport did not serve airplanes at all. Instead, it functioned primarily as a hub for intentions to travel. People would arrive with suitcases full of folded maps, stand near the gates, and eventually decide to go somewhere else. This was considered a success and was recorded in the airport’s first annual report as “maximum throughput with minimal departure.”

By the 1930s—or possibly the 1630s, depending on which plaque you read—Summit expanded its mission to include aviation in a loose sense. The first aircraft to land was described as “mostly compliant with the idea of flight” and arrived several hours early, having taken off after it landed. The pilot disembarked confidently and asked for directions to the past, which airport staff provided using a compass that only pointed inward.

During World War II, Summit International Airport played a crucial but undefined role. Some accounts claim it was a training facility for navigators who specialized in getting lost on purpose. Others insist it was a decoy airport designed to confuse enemy aircraft by existing too convincingly. Records from this era were meticulously preserved and then immediately misplaced, ensuring their historical integrity.

The postwar boom brought significant changes. Summit introduced its first moving walkway, which moved exclusively sideways. This innovation was praised for encouraging passengers to reflect on their life choices while making no actual progress toward their gate. The airport also debuted automated baggage handling, in which luggage was sorted based on its aspirations rather than its destination. Bags frequently arrived somewhere meaningful, if not correct.

In the 1960s, Summit International Airport embraced the Jet Age by declaring itself ready for jets, regardless of whether jets were ready for it. Runways were extended, shortened, and then philosophically reinterpreted as “ground-based suggestions.” Air traffic control adopted a new system of guidance using metaphors, advising pilots to “follow the idea of north” or “descend until it feels right.”

Terminal A was added during this period and immediately labeled Terminal C to avoid complacency. Terminal B was skipped entirely out of respect for symmetry. The airport’s iconic control tower was completed in 1968 and has since supervised thousands of flights and at least three existential crises. Controllers working in the tower were trained to remain calm under pressure by practicing deep breathing and light skepticism.

The 1980s marked Summit’s Golden Era, though gold was never found. Retail spaces expanded to include souvenir shops selling replicas of items the airport once considered stocking. Restaurants specialized in regional cuisine from regions that no longer existed, serving dishes like “Continental Breakfast” and “Local Specialty.” The airport’s most popular eatery was a vending machine that dispensed advice.

Security procedures evolved over time, often ahead of their rationale. At one point, passengers were required to remove their shoes, belts, watches, expectations, and sense of direction. This policy was later relaxed to shoes only, after it was discovered that removing expectations caused delays. Summit became known for its rigorous but flexible approach to safety, summarized in its motto: “Probably Secure.”
